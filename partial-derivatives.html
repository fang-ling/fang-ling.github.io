Partial Derivatives

1 Directional derivative and the gradient

  • Property 1: The directional derivative $\frac{df}{ds}$ in any given direction is the scalar projection of $\nabla f$ in that direction.

  • Property 2: The vector $\nabla f$ points in the direction in which $f$ increases most rapidly.
  • Property 3: The length of the vector $\nabla f$ is the maximum rate of increase of $f$.
  • Property 4: The gradient of a function $f(x,y,z)$ at a point $P_0$ is normal to the level surface of $f$ that passes through $P_0$.

2 Total differential

$$\begin{aligned}dz&=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}dx+\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}dy\\&=\nabla f\cdot(dx,dy)\end{aligned}$$

Example 1: Let $z=(1+xy)^y$, find $dz|_{(1,1)}$.

By definition, $dz=\nabla z\cdot(dx,dy)$.

To find the partial derivative of $z$ with respect to $x$, we can think of $y$ as a constant and differentiate in the usual way:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=y(1+xy)^{y-1}y$$

When we regard $x$ as a constant and use the technique of implicit differentiation to differentiate with respect to $y$, we obtain:

$$\ln z=\ln(1+xy)^y=y\ln(1+xy)$$ Then: $$\frac{\partial z}{z}=\partial y\ln(1+xy)+\frac{yx\partial y}{1+xy}$$ That is: $$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=\left(\ln(1+xy)+\frac{xy}{1+xy}\right)(1+xy)^y$$

Now we can calculate then final answer: $$dz=\left(y(1+xy)^{y-1}y\right)dx+\left(\left(\ln(1+xy)+\frac{xy}{1+xy}\right)(1+xy)^y\right)dy$$ $$dz|_{(1,1)}=dx+(2\ln2+1)dy$$

Example 2: Given $x=ze^{y+z}$, find $dz$ at $(e,0)$.

By using the same approach as described in the previous example, we can calculate $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$: $$\partial x=e^ye^z\partial z+ze^ze^y\partial z$$ $$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=\frac1{e^ye^z(1+z)}$$

Similarly, for $\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$:

$$0=e^ye^z\partial z+ze^ye^z\partial y+ze^ye^z\partial z$$ $$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=-\frac{ze^ye^z}{e^ye^z+ze^ye^z}=-\frac{z}{1+z}$$

Solving for $z$ at point $(e,0)$, we have $z=1$.

Then: $$dz=\nabla\cdot(dx,dy)=\frac{dx}{e^{y+z}(1+z)}-\frac{zdy}{1+z}$$ $$dz|_{(e,0)}=\frac{dx}{2e}-\frac{dy}2$$

Example 3: Given that $dz=(x^2+2xy-y^2)dx+(x^2-2xy-y^2)dy$, find $z(x,y)$.

By definition, we have: $$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=x^2+2xy-y^2$$ $$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=x^2-2xy-y^2$$

Integrating the first formula with respect to $x$, we obtain:

$$z=\int\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=\frac{x^3}3+x^2y-y^2x+u(y)$$

Differentiating both sides with respect to $y$, we get:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=0+x^2-2yx+u'(y)$$

Comparing two equations of $\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$, we find:

$$u'(y)=-y^2$$

That is, $u(y)=-\frac{y^3}3+C$. And now we have:

$$z=\frac{x^3}3+x^2y-y^2x-\frac{y^3}3+C$$

Example 4: Find the tangent plane and the normal line to the surface $$z=x^2+y^2-1$$ at the point $(2,1,4)$.

Let $F(x,y,z)=x^2+y^2-z-1$.

By definition, we have the normal vector $$\begin{aligned}\textbf n&=\nabla F|_{(2,1,4)}\\ &=(2x,2y,-1)|_{(2,1,4)}\\ &=(4,2,-1)\end{aligned}$$

So the equation of the tangent plane is $$4(x-1)+2(y-1)-(z-4)=0$$ or $$4x+2y-z=6$$

And the equation of the normal line is $$\frac{x-2}4=\frac{y-1}2=\frac{z-4}{-1}$$
