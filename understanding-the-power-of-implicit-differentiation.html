Understanding the Power of Implicit Differentiation

Undergraduate  •  Guitar Player  •  Gear  •  GitHub
  1. Implicit differentiation provides a way to find the derivative of inverse functions.

Example: Find $(\arcsin x)'$.

Solution:

Let $y=\arcsin x$, then $x=\sin y$, and differentiate both sides, we get:

$$\begin{aligned}\cos y dy&=dx\\\frac{dy}{dx}&=\frac1{\cos y}\end{aligned}$$

We can draw a right triangle to help us solve for $y$:

This yields:

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac1{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$$

  1. Implicit differentiation can also help us solve some difficult differentiations.

Example 2: Let $y=(1+x^2)^{\sin x}$. Find $dy/dx$.

Solution:

Take the logarithm of both sides and differentiate both sides:

$$\begin{aligned}\ln y&=\sin x\ln(1+x^2)\\ \frac{dy}y&=\left(\cos x\ln(1+x^2)+\frac{2x\sin x}{1+x^2}\right)dx\\ \frac{dy}{dx}&=y\times\left(\cos x\ln(1+x^2)+\frac{2x\sin x}{1+x^2}\right)\\ &=(1+x^2)^{\sin x}\left(\cos x\ln(1+x^2)+\frac{2x\sin x}{1+x^2}\right) \end{aligned}$$

Example 3: Let $y=\ln\frac1{\sqrt{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}}}$. Find $dy/dx$.

Solution:

Take the exponential of both sides and differentiate both sides, which yields:

$$\begin{aligned}e^y&=\frac1{\sqrt{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}}}=\left(x+(x^2+1)^{\frac12}\right)^{-\frac12}\\ e^ydy&=-\frac12\left(x+(x^2+1)^{\frac12}\right)^{-\frac32}\left(1+\frac12(x^2+1)^{-\frac12}\times2x\right)dx\\ \frac{dy}{dx}&=\frac{-\frac12\left(x+(x^2+1)^{\frac12}\right)^{-\frac32}\left(1+\frac12(x^2+1)^{-\frac12}\times2x\right)}{e^y}\\ &=\frac{-\frac12\left(x+(x^2+1)^{\frac12}\right)^{-\frac32}\left(1+\frac12(x^2+1)^{-\frac12}\times2x\right)}{\left(x+(x^2+1)^{\frac12}\right)^{-\frac12}}\\ &=-\frac12\times\frac{1+\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}}{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}}\\ &=-\frac12\times\frac{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}\times\frac{1}{x+\sqrt{x^2+1}}\\ &=-\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x^2+1}} \end{aligned}$$
