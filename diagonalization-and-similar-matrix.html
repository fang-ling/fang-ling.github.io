Introduction

Diagonalization: Suppose the $n$ by $n$ matrix $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $x_1,...,x_n$. Put them into the columns of an eigenvector matrix $X$. Then $X^{-1}AX$ is the eigenvalue matrix $\Lambda$:

$$X^{-1}AX=\Lambda=\begin{bmatrix}\lambda_1&&\\&\ddots&\\&&\lambda_n\end{bmatrix}$$

Every matrix $C=B^{-1}AB$ has the same eigenvalues as A. These $C$'s are "similar" to $A$.

Because matrices are similar if and only if they represent the same linear operator with respect to (possibly) different bases, similar matrices share all properties of their shared underlying operator:

  • Rank
  • Characteristic polynomial, and attributes that can be derived from it:
    • Determinant
    • Trace
    • Eigenvalues, and their algebraic multiplicities
  • ...

Example 1: Given that $A$ is similar to $B$, find an invertible matrix $P$ such that $P^{-1}AP=B$. $$A=\begin{bmatrix}2&0&0\\0&0&1\\0&1&0\end{bmatrix},\quad B=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&-1&0\\0&-6&2\end{bmatrix}$$

For $A$, we can calculate $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ to find all the eigenvalues.

$$\begin{vmatrix}2-\lambda&0&0\\0&-\lambda&1\\0&1&-\lambda\end{vmatrix}=0$$

This gives us:

$$(2-\lambda)(\lambda^2-1)=0$$

or $\lambda_1=2,\lambda_2=1,\lambda_3=-1$, and the eigenvalue matrix $\Lambda$: $$\Lambda=\begin{bmatrix}2&&\\&1&\\&&-1\end{bmatrix}$$

Since $B$ is similar to $A$, they share the same eigenvalues and eigenvalue matrix.

The next step is to find the eigenvectors. For $A$, we use the equation $(A-\lambda I)x=0$. To solve this, we first reduce $A-\lambda I$ to its row echelon form and then find the special solutions to $(A-\lambda I)x=0$ by choosing the free variable to be $1$. This gives us three independent eigenvectors $x_1=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix},x_2=\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\1\end{bmatrix},x_3=\begin{bmatrix}0\\-1\\1\end{bmatrix}$ corresponding to the eigenvalue $\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3$.

Similarly, for $B$, we obtain $y_1=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix},y_2=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix},y_3=\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\2\end{bmatrix}$.

Then:

$$X^{-1}AX=\Lambda=Y^{-1}BY$$ $$YX^{-1}AX=BY$$ $$YX^{-1}AXY^{-1}=B$$ $$(XY^{-1})^{-1}AXY^{-1}=B$$

That is,

$$\begin{aligned}P&=XY^{-1}\\ &=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&-1\\0&1&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0&1&0\\0&0&1\\1&0&2\end{bmatrix}^{-1}\\ &=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&-1\\0&1&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0&-2&1\\1&0&0\\0&1&0\end{bmatrix}\\ &=\begin{bmatrix}0&-2&1\\1&-1&0\\1&1&0\end{bmatrix}\end{aligned}$$
