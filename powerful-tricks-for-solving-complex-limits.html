Powerful Tricks for Solving Complex Limits

Undergraduate  •  Guitar Player  •  Gear  •  GitHub

1 Taylor's theorem

$$f(x)=\sum_{k=0}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$$

Example 1: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}x$.

Solution: Expanding $\sin x$ in terms of the first-order Taylor series, we have: $$\sin x\approx x$$ Then, $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}x=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x}{x}=1$$

Example 2: Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln\left(\sin^2x+e^x\right)-x}{\ln\left(e^{2x}-x^2\right)-2x}$$

Solution: $$\begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln\left(\sin^2x+e^x\right)-x}{\ln\left(e^{2x}-x^2\right)-2x}&=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln\left(\sin^2x+e^x\right)-\ln e^x}{\ln\left(e^{2x}-x^2\right)-\ln e^{2x}}\\ &=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln\left(\frac{\sin^2x+e^x}{e^x}\right)}{\ln\left(\frac{e^{2x}-x^2}{e^{2x}}\right)}\\ &=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln\left(1+\frac{\sin^2x}{e^x}\right)}{\ln\left(1-\frac{x^2}{e^{2x}}\right)}\\ \text{by using $\ln(x+1)\approx x$, we get:}\\ &=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\frac{\sin^2x}{e^x}}{-\frac{x^2}{e^{2x}}}\\ &=-\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x\sin^2x}{x^2}\\ \text{substituting $\sin x\approx x$ yields:}\\ &=-\lim_{x\to0}e^x\\ &=-1 \end{aligned}$$

2 L'Hospital's Rule

$$\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

3 Indeterminate form: $1^{\infty}$

Step 1: Rewrite the problem as $e$ raised to the natural logarithm of your function, since $a^b=e^{\ln a^b}=e^{b\ln a}$.

Step 2: (optional) Use Taylor's theorem to simply the natural logarithm of your function if possible. For example, when near zero, we have: $$\ln(x+1)\approx x$$

Example 3: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to0}(1-x^2)^\frac1{1-\sqrt{1-x^2}}$.

Solution:

$$\begin{aligned} \lim_{x\to0}(1-x^2)^\frac1{1-\sqrt{1-x^2}}&=\lim_{x\to0}e^\frac{\ln(1-x^2)}{1-\sqrt{1-x^2}}\\ &=e^{\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\ln((-x^2)+1)}{1-\sqrt{1-x^2}}}\\ &=e^{\lim_{x\to0}\frac{-x^2}{1-\sqrt{1-x^2}}}\\ \text{by L'Hospital's rule, we have:}\\ &=e^{\lim_{x\to0}\frac{-2x}{-\frac12(1-x^2)^{-\frac12}(-2x)}}\\ &=e^{-2} \end{aligned}$$

Example 4: Evaluate $\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{1+\sqrt[n]3}2\right)^n$.

Solution:

At the point near zero, we have: $a^x\approx 1+x\ln a$. Since: $$a^x\approx \frac{a^0}{0!}x^0+\frac{a^0\ln a}{1!}x^1$$ This yields: $$\begin{aligned} \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{1+\sqrt[n]3}2\right)^n&=\lim_{n\to\infty}e^{n\ln\left(\frac{1+\sqrt[n]3}2\right)}\\ &=\exp\left({\lim_{n\to\infty}n\ln\left(\left(\frac{1+\sqrt[n]3}2-1\right)+1\right)}\right)\\ &=\exp\left({\lim_{n\to\infty}n\left(\frac{1+3^\frac1n}2-1\right)}\right)\\ \text{by $a^x\approx x\ln a+1$, we obtain:}\\ &=\exp\left({\lim_{n\to\infty}n\left(\frac{1+1+\frac{\ln3}n}2-1\right)}\right)\\ &=\exp\left(\frac12\ln3\right)\\ &=e^{\ln\sqrt3}\\ &=\sqrt3 \end{aligned}$$

Example 5: Evaluate $\lim_{x\to0}\frac{(1+x)^{\frac3x}-e^3}x$.

Solution:

At the point near zero, we have: $e^x-1\approx x$. Since: $$e^x\approx \frac{e^0}{0!}x^0+\frac{e^0}{1!}x^1$$

Now we get:

$$\begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to0}\frac{(1+x)^{\frac3x}-e^3}x&=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^{\frac3x\ln(1+x)}-e^3}x\\ &=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^3\left(e^{\frac3x\ln(1+x)-3}-1\right)}x\\ \text{by $e^x-1\approx x$, we get:}\\ &=e^3\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\frac3x\ln(1+x)-3}x\\ &=e^3\lim_{x\to0}\frac{3\ln(1+x)-3x}{x^2}\\ &=e^3\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\frac3{1+x}-3}{2x}\\ &=3e^3\lim_{x\to0}\frac{-\frac1{(1+x)^2}}2\\ &=-\frac32e^3 \end{aligned}$$

4 Limits of series

There are two basic methods: using the Squeeze theorem or calculating it.

Example 6: Evaluate $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+k}$$

Solution:

$$\begin{aligned} \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+1}&\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+k}&\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+n}\\ \end{aligned}$$
Since
$$\sum_{k=1}^n k=\sum_1^{n+1}x\delta x=\left[\frac12x^{\underline2}\right]_1^{n+1}=\left[\frac{x(x-1)}2\right]_1^{n+1}=\frac{(n+1)n}2$$
We have:
$$\begin{aligned} \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac {(n+1)n}{2n^2+2n+2}&\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+k}\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac {(n+1)n}{2n^2+2n+2n}\\ \frac12&\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+k}\leq\frac12 \end{aligned}$$
Therefore: $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac k{n^2+n+k}=\frac12$$

Example 7: Evaluate $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1k}$$

Solution: $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]1\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1k}\leq\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]n$$

It's trivial to see that $\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]1=1$. Let's prove it:

$$\begin{aligned}\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]n&=\lim_{n\to\infty}n^{\frac1n}\\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty}e^{\frac1n\ln n}\\ &=e^{\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\ln n}n}\\ &=e^0\\ &=1\end{aligned}$$

And therefore $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1k}=1$$

Example 8: Evaluate $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1{4k^2-1}$$

Solution: $$\begin{aligned}\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1{4k^2-1}&=\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\frac1{(2k-1)(2k+1)}\\ &=\frac12\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n\left(\frac1{2k-1}-\frac1{2k+1}\right)\\ &=\frac12\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1-\frac13+\frac13-\frac15+...-\frac1{2n+1}\right)\\ &=\frac12\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1-\frac1{2n+1}\right)\\ &=\frac12 \end{aligned}$$
