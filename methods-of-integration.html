The Method of Substitution

  • Example: Find $\int xe^{-x^2}dx$.
  • Solution: $\int xe^{-x^2}dx=-\frac12\int e^{-x^2}d(-x^2)=\frac12e^{-x^2}+C$

Trigonometric Substitution

An integral involving one of the radical expressions $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$,$\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$,$\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$ (where $a$ is a positive constant) can often be transformed into a familiar trigonometric integral by using a suitable trigonometric substitution. There are three cases, which depend on the trigonometric identities $$\begin{aligned}1-\sin^2\theta=\cos^2\theta\\ 1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta\\ \sec^2\theta-1=\tan^2\theta\end{aligned}$$

For example: $$ \begin{aligned} \int\frac{\sqrt{x^2-a^2}}x dx&\stackrel{x = a\sec\theta,dx=a\sec\theta\tan\theta d\theta}{=}\int\frac{a\tan\theta}{a\sec\theta}\times a\sec\theta\tan\theta d\theta\\&=a\int\tan^2\theta d\theta\\&=a\int(\sec^2\theta-1)d\theta\\&=a\tan\theta-a\theta+C\\&=\sqrt{x^2-a^2}-a\arctan\frac{\sqrt{x^2-a^2}}a+C \end{aligned}$$

Completing The Square

We can extend trigonometric substitution to integrals involving general quadratic polynomials and their square roots, that is, expression of the form $ax^2+bx+c$ and $\sqrt{ax^2+bx+c}$. The process of completing the square is based on the simple fact that $$(x+A)^2=x^2+2Ax+A^2$$

For example:

$$\begin{aligned}\int\frac{(x+2)dx}{\sqrt{-x^2+2x+3}}&=\int\frac{(x+2)dx}{\sqrt{4-(x-1)^2}}\\&=\int\frac{(x-1+3)d(x-1)}{\sqrt{4-(x-1)^2}}\\&=\int\frac{(x-1)d(x-1)}{\sqrt{4-(x-1)^2}}+3\int\frac{d(x-1)}{\sqrt{4-(x-1)^2}}\\&=-\sqrt{4-(x-1)^2}+3\arcsin\left(\frac{x-1}2\right)+C\end{aligned}$$

Trigonometric Functions

The double-angle and half-angle formulas can be used to simplify complicated trigonometric expressions and solve trigonometric integrals.

The double-angle formula is: $$\begin{aligned}\sin2x&=2\sin x\cos x\\\cos2x&=\cos^2x-\sin^2x\end{aligned}$$

Together with the trigonometric identity $\cos^2x+\sin^2x=1$, we can solve to the half-angle formula: $$\begin{aligned}\cos^2x&=\frac{1+\cos2x}2\\\sin^2x&=\frac{1-\cos2x}2\end{aligned}$$

Let's look at an example of how we can use these formulas to solve a trigonometric integral.

Example: Find $\int\sin^2x\cos^4xdx$

Solution: $$\begin{aligned}\int\sin^2x\cos^4xdx&=\frac14\int\sin^22x\cos^2xdx\\&=\frac14\int\sin^22x\frac{1+\cos2x}2dx\\&=\frac18\int\sin^22xdx+\frac1{16}\int\sin^22xd(\sin2x)\\&=\frac1{16}\frac13\sin^32x+\frac1{16}\int(1-\cos^4x)dx\\&=\frac1{16}(x-\frac14\sin^4x+\frac13\sin^32x)+C\end{aligned}$$

The Method of Partial Fractions

Example: Find $\int\frac{6x^2+14x-20}{x^3-4x}dx$.

Solution: We can factor the denominator by writing $x^3-4x=x(x+2)(x-2)$. Accordingly, we have a decomposition of the form $$\frac{6x^2+14x-20}{x^3-4x}=\frac{6x^2+14x-20}{x(x+2)(x-2)}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x+2}+\frac{C}{x-2}$$ for certain constants $A$, $B$ and $C$. To find these constants we can either use the cover-up method or the method of undetermined coefficient, which yields $$\frac{6x^2+14x-20}{x^3-4x}=\frac5x-\frac3{x+2}+\frac4{x-2}$$ We therefore have $$\int\frac{6x^2+14x-20}{x^3-4x}dx=5\ln x-3\ln(x+2)+4\ln(x-2)+C$$

Note that if we have to integrate an improper rational function, it is essential to begin by performing long division until we reach a remainder whose degree is less than that of the denominator.

Integration by Parts

When the formula for the derivative of a product (the product rule) is written in the notation of differentials, it is $$d(uv)=udv+vdu$$ or $$udv=d(uv)-vdu$$ and by integrating we obtain $$\int udv=uv-\int vdu$$

Example: Find $\int x\cos xdx$.

$$\begin{aligned}\int x\cos xdx=&\int xd(\sin x)\\=&x\sin x-\int\sin xdx\\=&x\sin x+\cos x+C\end{aligned}$$
